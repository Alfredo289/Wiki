Summary

YB describes a Claude + Obsidian workflow they’ve used for 9+ months, arguing it fundamentally changed their daily habits. The thread distinguishes this personal thinking use case from andrej-karpathy's llm-wiki-pattern, which YB considers better suited for research projects with synthetic artifacts. The key difference: for personal thinking, you can’t outsource your own life experiences to AI - the personal context you add is what makes the tool useful.

Five Practices

1. Commonplace Practice

Daily habit of jotting down observations, epiphanies, thoughts, and questions. Notes don’t need to be clean or structured. Voice transcription recommended because it captures more context, including uncertainty (“ifs, buts, and ums”). The claim: context compounds over weeks and months, producing surprising pattern recognition.

2. Atomic Notes (via Andy Matuschak)

Notes should be concepts, not facts/names/dates. YB frames them as “API endpoints you call when solving problems.” Thinking in atomic notes lets you pull context from different life domains you’d normally keep separate. References Paul Graham's “writing is thinking” - write notes for yourself, not for an audience.

The payoff: once you have enough atomic notes, you can dictate a train of thought and Claude pulls your own past thinking to help.

3. Intentional Reading (via Readwise)

Connects Readwise or Obsidian Web Clipper to build a reading pipeline. YB’s key insight: much past content consumption was “cope” - feeling productive while passively scrolling. The new approach: search for potential reads during scroll time, then dedicate focused time to reading with highlighting and note-taking. Even a 5-minute article should produce new atomic notes.

References @phokarlsson’s “Structure your milieu” concept - deliberately curate who gets access to your brain.

4. Agentic Constitution

A .md file that serves as the portal between Obsidian and Claude. Contains high-level context: who you are, your big question, how you like to work, main goals, current problems, team context. Agents read this file first before doing any work.

Key characteristic: it’s a constant WIP document, updated weekly, treated like updating a team on changing priorities.

5. Knowledge Work PRs

Vault subroutines - specific instruction sets that start by reading the agentic constitution. Example: “Tend the Vault” checks daily commonplace entries, identifies threads, drafts updates to atomic notes. The user then reviews changes in a voice session, approving/tweaking/rejecting, similar to a code PR review process.

Counter-arguments

  • The 9-month claim is a single practitioner’s experience with no controlled comparison
  • Voice transcription produces messy notes that may create noise rather than signal for LLMs
  • The “context compounds” claim is unfalsifiable - confirmation bias could explain perceived patterns
  • Heavy setup cost and daily habit requirement may not suit most people

Data gaps

  • No specifics on what “surprising patterns” the system surfaced
  • No comparison of outcomes vs simpler note-taking approaches
  • The “Tend the Vault” subroutine and other agent workflows are described but not shown
  • No discussion of failure modes or when the system breaks down
