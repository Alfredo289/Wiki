A note-taking practice where each note captures a single concept rather than facts, names, or dates. Attributed to Andy Matuschak's evergreen notes practice, itself building on Niklas Luhmann’s Zettelkasten method.

YB (yb-effect-2026-commonplace-practice) frames atomic notes as “API endpoints you call when solving problems” - reusable conceptual building blocks that can be combined across life domains you’d normally keep separate.

In an LLM-augmented vault, atomic notes serve as the structured layer that makes commonplace-practice captures useful. Once enough atomic notes exist, you can dictate a train of thought and the AI pulls relevant past thinking from across the vault.

Connects to Paul Graham's observation that “writing is thinking” - the act of distilling a concept into an atomic note forces clarity.

Counter-arguments

  • Atomicity is subjective - practitioners disagree on the right granularity
  • The “API endpoint” metaphor may overstate how cleanly concepts decompose
  • Overhead of maintaining many small notes can become its own burden

Data gaps

  • Matuschak’s original writing not yet ingested as a primary source
  • No evidence on optimal note count before LLM recall becomes useful
  • Single source describing the practice in an LLM context
